Ls 1- READING AND ANALYSIS OF MAPS
I. Keywords:-
1. Cartography: The science of making maps. Someone who makes or draw maps is called a
cartographer. Anaximander was a Greek cartographer.
2. Thematic maps: A map which focuses only on one aspect. Such maps are called ‘Thematic Maps’.
Ex: Temperature map or rainfall distribution.
3. Projection: A systematic transformation of the latitudes and longitudes of locations from the
surface of the globe into locations on a plane surface.
4. Atlas: A collection of maps usually arranged according to different themes is known an atlas.
5. Contour: Contours are lines on the map joining places of same height-measured from the sea
level.
II. Answer the following questions.
1. Can you point out the differences between a map and a photograph?
Ans: a) A map represents some features that are considered important by the cartographers but a
photograph presents a realistic image.
b) A map is prepared based on certain themes and so, it gives us details of a place and we can read it
with the help of the elements used but a photograph may not be able to show the features of a
region like a map.
2. Define Physical Maps.
Ans: Physical maps are designed to show the natural landscape features of the earth like the
mountains, rivers, hills, plateaus etc.
3. What are contour lines used for?
Ans: a) Contour lines give an indication of the slope of the land as well as the elevation above sea
level.
b) Whenever contour lines are far apart, it represents a gentle slope and the closer lines represent
steep slope and uniformly spaced lines represent uniform slope.
III. Answer the following questions.
4. Why do you think the colonial powers invest so much money to prepare detailed maps?
Ans: a) They believed that it was essential to get accurate knowledge about the land that they were
going to rule.
b) It was only through mapping the colonial powers could get information about the resources.
c) Through mapping they could enable their powers to establish their rule over and to exploit the
resources and to know the landforms and the tribes living in the territories.
5. Write the uses of Aerial photography and Satellite imagery?
Ans: Aerial photography is used in cartography, planning of land usage, environmental studies etc.
With the use of sophisticated range of cameras information is collected on geology, agricultural
management, forestry, water pollution, natural disasters, wildlife management etc.
Satellite images are used to measure, identify and track human activity. Today, nations use
information derived from the satellite imagery for government decision making, civil defence
operations, police and Geographic Information Systems (GIS) in general.
6. Do you think the use of maps has changed from the time of ancient to present days? In what way
do you find similarities and differences?
Ans: Similarities :-
a) Maps in the ancient period were not accurate. They were used as an important tool.
b) The maps were mostly symbolic representation of the earth. Maps in ancient period and now are
used for trade and voyage.
c) They are also used to identify army targets not only now but also during the ancient time.
Differences :-
a) In ancient period maps were prepared on the basis of travels and descriptions heard from but
now they are prepared on the basis of accurate data provided by the surveys and satellite imageries.
b) In olden days maps were used for sailing, trade, conquests, colonising and for fighting wars but
today maps are used for planning and development for countries.
